The Founder's Playbook

15 years of company building distilled into systematic frameworks. This is how you turn entrepreneurial intuition into operational science.

"Most people think company building is art. I've proven it's science. When you understand the algorithms behind entrepreneurial success, you don't guess—you architect."
01

The Leverage Equation

Framework: L = (I × S × A) / R

Leverage = (Intelligence × Speed × Automation) / Resources

Every decision I make runs through this filter. Intelligence means understanding the true constraints. Speed means bias toward action over analysis. Automation means building systems that scale without you. Resources include time, capital, and energy—all finite. The companies that win maximize this equation relentlessly.

Case Study: At Mako Fire, we increased our leverage ratio 300% by automating compliance workflows. Same team, 3x output, 1/3 the errors.
02

First Principles Revenue

Framework: Revenue = Value Created × Value Captured × Distribution

Most founders optimize for one variable. Winners optimize the system.

Value Created is your product-market fit depth. Value Captured is your pricing power and business model efficiency. Distribution is your ability to reach customers at scale. I've seen too many brilliant products fail because founders didn't architect all three simultaneously.

Applied: Built Mako Fire's revenue engine by creating regulatory value (deep), capturing it through retainer models (efficient), and distributing through Fortune 500 relationships (scalable).
03

The Compound Decision Framework

Every Decision Is Either Linear or Exponential

Linear decisions add value. Exponential decisions multiply it.

I categorize every major decision into four quadrants: High Impact/Reversible, High Impact/Irreversible, Low Impact/Reversible, Low Impact/Irreversible. 80% of CEO time should be spent on High Impact/Irreversible decisions. Everything else should be delegated or systematized.

Example: Choosing our first Fortune 500 client was High Impact/Irreversible—it defined our market positioning for years. Hiring individual team members was High Impact/Reversible—important but correctable.
04

Systems Architecture Mindset

Build the Machine That Builds the Machine

First-order thinking: build a company. Second-order thinking: build the system that builds companies.

Every process I create is designed to be documentation, training, and automation-ready. Every hire strengthens the system rather than creating dependencies. Every client success creates repeatable methodologies. This is how you scale from operator to orchestrator.

Result: Our fire protection methodology became so systematized that client compliance improved 80% with half the manual oversight. The system scaled beyond my direct involvement.
05

The Constraint Optimization Protocol

Theory of Constraints Applied to Startups

Identify → Exploit → Subordinate → Elevate → Repeat

Evolution: Our constraint progression: Talent acquisition → Client pipeline → Delivery capacity → Geographic expansion → Exit preparation. Solving them in sequence unlocked exponential growth.
06

Pattern Recognition as Competitive Advantage

Experience + Reflection = Wisdom

But only if you systematize the pattern recognition process.

I maintain decision logs, outcome analyses, and pattern databases. When I see a situation, I'm not just drawing on intuition—I'm accessing 15 years of categorized, analyzed experiences. This is how you develop founder-level judgment at enterprise scale.

Application: My pattern recognition in M&A negotiations came from categorizing every previous deal structure, outcome, and counterparty behavior. By acquisition time, I was reading moves three steps ahead.
